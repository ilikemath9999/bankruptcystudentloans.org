Student Loans & Bankruptcy in Washington

How student loan discharge works in WA bankruptcy - the Brunner test, adversary proceedings, the 2022 DOJ/DOE undue hardship guidance, and 9th Circuit authority.

The Brunner Test in the 9th Circuit

Student loans are presumptively non-dischargeable under 11 U.S.C. Section 523(a)(8) unless the debtor proves "undue hardship" in an adversary proceeding. Most circuits, including the 9th Circuit, apply the Brunner test from Brunner v. New York State Higher Education Services Corp., 831 F.2d 395 (2d Cir. 1987). The test requires:

  • The debtor cannot maintain a minimal standard of living while repaying the loans
  • Additional circumstances suggest the state of affairs will persist for a significant portion of the repayment period
  • The debtor has made good faith efforts to repay

See full Brunner test explanation.

DOJ/DOE Undue Hardship Guidance (November 2022)

In November 2022, the Department of Justice and Department of Education issued joint guidance on student loan discharge under Section 523(a)(8). The guidance establishes a streamlined process for federal student loans where the DOJ will support discharge if the debtor:

  • Cannot presently afford to repay the loan based on current income and expenses
  • Inability to pay is likely to persist
  • Good faith efforts to repay have been made (or reasonable attempts, considering the loan history)

The guidance uses an attestation form and a standardized analysis by DOJ. This has materially increased student loan discharge success rates in cases where the guidance applies.

U.S. Trustee Region 18 in Washington processes cases consistent with the guidance. Filers should review the attestation form and the guidance before filing an adversary proceeding.

Adversary Proceedings in WA

Student loan discharge requires filing an adversary proceeding, a separate lawsuit within the bankruptcy case, against the loan holder (and typically the Department of Education for federal loans).

Key steps:

  1. File the complaint under Rule 7001 and Section 523(a)(8)
  2. Serve the complaint on the loan holder and, for federal loans, the DOE and U.S. Attorney
  3. Demonstrate each Brunner prong with evidence (income, expenses, medical records, repayment history)
  4. For federal loans post-November 2022, complete the DOJ attestation form
  5. Trial or settlement on the Brunner record

See adversary proceeding mechanics.

Chapter 13 Treatment

In Chapter 13, student loans are typically paid pro-rata with other unsecured debt through the plan. The debtor may still file an adversary proceeding for discharge on undue hardship grounds, either during the case or after completion of the plan.

Advantages of the Chapter 13 route in some Washington cases:

  • Automatic stay stops collection during the plan
  • Accrued interest and penalties may be reduced
  • A post-plan discharge adversary may succeed where a pre-plan one would not

See Chapter 13 and student loans.

Attorney Fee Considerations

Student loan adversary proceedings typically carry fees in addition to the base Chapter 7 or 13 attorney fee. Typical range $2,000-$6,000 depending on case complexity and whether the loan holder contests discharge.

Under 11 U.S.C. Section 329(b), the court may review any attorney fee paid "in connection with" the bankruptcy case, including fees paid for adversary proceedings. Any fee paid for student loan litigation must be disclosed under Rule 2016(b) and is subject to the reasonableness standard. See Section 329 fee disgorgement overview.
